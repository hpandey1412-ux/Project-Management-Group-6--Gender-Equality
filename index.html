1.1 Implicit Bias Theory

Implicit bias refers to attitudes or stereotypes that affect decisions and actions unconsciously (Greenwald & Banaji, 1995). Unlike explicit prejudice, these associations operate below conscious awareness - meaning even well-intentioned managers are susceptible.

The Implicit Association Test (IAT), developed by Greenwald, McGhee & Schwartz (1998), measures the strength of associations between concepts (e.g. male–science, female–arts) via reaction time differences. Data from over 4.5 million IAT completions show consistent male-leadership and female-family associations across cultures (Project Implicit, 2023).

📌 Key Study: Moss-Racusin et al. (2012)

Science faculty at US research universities were asked to evaluate a student's CV for a laboratory manager position. The CVs were identical except for the applicant's name (Jennifer vs. John). Results:

  • "Jennifer" rated significantly less competent and less hireable
  • Offered a starting salary of $26,508 vs. $30,238 for "John"
  • Effect was statistically significant and occurred in both male and female faculty
  • Published in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences
ECCSR: Ethical SDG 5.1

1.2 Social Role Theory (Eagly, 1987)

Eagly's Social Role Theory proposes that gender differences in behaviour arise from the roles society assigns to men and women. These role expectations generate descriptive norms ("what women are like") and prescriptive norms ("what women should be like").

In the workplace, this creates a double bind for women in leadership: if a woman displays communal traits (warm, collaborative), she is seen as likeable but not competent as a leader; if she displays agentic traits (assertive, decisive), she is seen as violating her gender role and faces backlash.

Communal Traits

Associated with women: warmth, empathy, cooperation. Valued socially but penalised in hiring for senior roles.

Agentic Traits

Associated with men: assertiveness, ambition, independence. Aligned with leadership prototypes - advantaging male candidates.

The Backlash Effect

Women who display agentic traits face social and professional penalties for "role violation" (Rudman & Glick, 2001).

Leadership Prototype Mismatch

Think Manager–Think Male (Schein, 1973): leadership roles are mentally associated with masculine attributes, disadvantaging female applicants at a subconscious level.

ECCSR: Creating SDG 5.5

1.3 Institutional Theory & Systemic Discrimination

DiMaggio & Powell (1983) explain that organisations are subject to institutional pressures, they mimic each other's structures (isomorphism), follow regulatory mandates, and conform to professional norms. This means gender inequality can be embedded in organisational design rather than arising solely from individual prejudice.

Systemic discrimination occurs when gender-neutral policies produce gendered outcomes. Examples include:

  • Informal "old boys' network" sponsorship that advantages men in promotion decisions
  • Performance criteria written around full-time availability, penalising part-time workers (disproportionately women)
  • Evaluation frameworks that reward attributes culturally coded as masculine
"Organisations are not neutral; they embody and reproduce existing social inequalities through their structures, cultures, and practices." : Acker, J. (1990). Hierarchies, Jobs, Bodies: A Theory of Gendered Organisations. Gender & Society

2.2 The Broken Rung

McKinsey's research identified that the most critical barrier is not the glass ceiling - it is the "broken rung": the first promotion from individual contributor to manager. For every 100 men promoted, only 87 women are promoted at this stage. This gap compounds over time.

📌 The Numbers Behind the Gap

  • For every 100 men promoted to manager, only 72 women of colour are promoted
  • Women are 18% less likely to be promoted than men with equivalent performance ratings
  • Men are promoted based on potential; women are promoted based on proven track record
  • Companies lose women at manager level faster than at any other point in the pipeline

Goldman Sachs: Systemic Exclusion at Scale

In 2010, Goldman Sachs faced a $215 million class-action lawsuit alleging systemic pay and promotion discrimination against women. Internally reviewed data showed that female vice presidents earned $100,000 less annually than male counterparts with equivalent tenure. The case became a landmark study in how structural promotion criteria - even when formally neutral - can embed gender disadvantage. You will conduct a full ECCSR analysis of this case in Module 5.

Common Myths vs. Evidence

"Women don't apply for senior roles because they lack confidence." +
Research by Tomas Chamorro-Premuzic (2013) shows that overconfidence in incompetent male leaders is systematically rewarded, creating the illusion that confidence = competence. Women are not less confident - they are penalised for confidence displays. The selection system itself mistakes male overconfidence for leadership potential.
"Gender gaps disappear when you control for role and experience." +
When you control for role, you are controlling for the very variable that bias affects. Women are disproportionately sorted into lower-paying, lower-prestige roles through biased assignment and promotion decisions. "Controlling for role" can mask, rather than explain, the discrimination. (Blau & Kahn, 2017)
"This is a pipeline problem - we just need to hire more women into entry roles." +
Women already enter organisations at roughly equal rates to men. The pipeline is not empty - it leaks. Focusing solely on entry-level recruitment ignores the structural barriers in promotion, sponsorship, performance review, and retention that filter women out before they reach senior levels. (McKinsey WitW, 2023)

3.1 Structural Interventions: What the Evidence Shows

🎭 Blind Recruitment +

Blind recruitment removes name, gender, and demographic identifiers from application materials before initial screening. The landmark study by Goldin & Rouse (2000) of symphony orchestras found that blind auditions increased the probability of a woman being hired by 25–46%.

Implementation steps:

  • Anonymise CV name and photo fields at submission
  • Remove university names where prestige bias is a risk
  • Pilot with a control group to measure impact on shortlist diversity
  • Apply to written work samples and case studies, not just CVs
ECCSR: Ethical SDG 5.5
📋 Structured Interviews & Behavioural Scoring +

Unstructured interviews allow bias to fill the vacuum of subjectivity. Structured interviews - where all candidates are asked the same questions and scored against a pre-defined rubric - reduce the variance explained by demographic characteristics.

Best practice scoring design:

  • Develop behavioural anchors (1–5 scale with specific descriptors) before interviews begin
  • Use STAR-format questions (Situation, Task, Action, Result)
  • Multiple independent scorers who do not confer until after individual ratings are recorded
  • Document all scores; provide written justifications for shortlisting decisions

Schmidt & Hunter (1998) meta-analysis: structured interviews have a validity coefficient of 0.51 vs 0.38 for unstructured - they are more predictive of performance and more equitable.

ECCSR: Results · Creating
👥 Diverse Interview Panels +

Panel composition matters. When all interviewers share similar backgrounds, affinity bias compounds. Research by Castilla (2008) shows that diversity in decision-making bodies significantly reduces discriminatory outcomes - but only when panels are genuinely empowered to challenge each other.

Minimum standards:

  • At least one woman on every shortlisting and interview panel
  • Cross-functional representation to avoid in-group affinity
  • Designated "bias spotter" role in panel - raises challenges in real time
  • Panel debrief structured around scored criteria, not impressionistic reaction
ECCSR: Collaboration SDG 16.7
💰 Pay Transparency & Structured Banding +

Pay transparency reduces the negotiation gap that disadvantages women (Bowles et al., 2007). When salary bands are publicly stated in job advertisements, women are not penalised for not negotiating - and the base rate for all candidates is reset to the market rate.

EU Pay Transparency Directive (2023): Requires employers with 100+ employees to report gender pay gaps annually and introduce pay remediation plans. Ireland is transposing this directive by 2026.

ECCSR: Sustainable · Results SDG 5.1
📊 Gender-Disaggregated Data & Representation Targets +

What gets measured gets managed. Organisations that track gender representation data at each stage of the talent process - application, shortlist, offer, acceptance, retention - are able to identify where bias is most acute.

Representation targets (not quotas): Targets set a clear aspiration without mandating a specific hire. Evidence from Norway's board quota (40% women mandated) showed a 12% improvement in firm financial performance (Ahern & Dittmar, 2012) alongside improved governance quality.

ECCSR: Results · Sustainable SDG 5.5

4.2 The Representation Audit Process

A systematic audit of your talent process reveals where and how bias enters. The audit follows a four-stage cycle:

1. Data Collection

Gather gender-disaggregated data at each pipeline stage: applications, screening, interviews, offers, acceptances, promotions, exits. Ensure GDPR compliance throughout.

2. Gap Analysis

Compare representation at each stage to the previous stage and to external benchmarks (sector averages, Catalyst benchmarks). Identify where the "leak" is greatest.

3. Root Cause Review

Qualitative interviews with HR, hiring managers, and candidates. Identify whether gaps stem from pipeline, process, or culture factors. Map to ECCSR framework.

4. Intervention & Re-audit

Implement targeted interventions from Module 3 toolkit. Set a 12-month re-audit date. Report findings to board and publish externally (where appropriate).

Primary Case Study

Goldman Sachs & the Systemic Pay and Promotion Gap (2010–2020)

Background: In 2010, a group of female Goldman Sachs employees filed a class-action lawsuit alleging systemic pay and promotion discrimination. The case, Cristina Chen-Oster et al. v. Goldman Sachs, alleged that Goldman's culture systematically excluded women from high-revenue businesses and that internal review processes were structurally biased.

Key Findings from Internal Data (disclosed during litigation):

  • Female VPs earned approximately $100,000 less annually than male VPs of equivalent tenure
  • Women received lower performance ratings despite equivalent client revenue generation
  • Annual 360-degree review processes used language more likely to penalise assertiveness in women
  • Promotion committees were overwhelmingly male; no structured criteria existed for manager-level advancement
  • The "360 Committee" review process lacked transparent scoring - decisions were made through informal consensus

Goldman Sachs settled the case for $215 million in 2023 without admitting liability, while committing to reforms in their promotion and compensation processes. The case remains a landmark in employment discrimination law.
